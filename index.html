Abstract

Vision-Language Models (VLMs) are increasingly used in clinical diagnostics, but their robustness to adversarial attacks is largely unexplored, posing serious risks. Existing medical image attacks mostly target secondary goals like model stealing or adversarial finetuning, while vanilla transferable attacks from natural images fail by introducing visible distortions that are easily detectable by clinicians. To address this, we propose MedFocusLeak, a novel and highly transferable black-box multimodal attack that forces incorrect medical diagnoses while ensuring perturbations remain imperceptible. The approach strategically introduces synergistic perturbations into non-diagnostic background regions of a medical image and uses an Attention-Distract loss to deliberately shift the model's diagnostic focus away from pathological areas. Through comprehensive evaluations on 6 distinct medical imaging modalities, we demonstrate that MedFocusLeak attains state-of-the-art effectiveness, producing adversarial examples that elicit plausible but incorrect diagnostic outputs across a range of VLMs. We also propose a novel evaluation framework with new metrics that capture both the success of the misleading text generation and the quality preservation of the medical image in one statistical number. Our findings expose a systematic weakness in the reasoning capabilities of contemporary VLMs in clinical settings.

How MedFocusLeak Works

MedFocusLeak integrates four technically grounded principles to generate stealthy, transferable adversarial medical images that corrupt model attention and induce clinically plausible misdiagnoses.

🎯

① Adversarial Text Generation

Given a medical image I and prompt x, the attacker crafts a targeted adversarial prompt to produce a plausible but incorrect diagnosis xadv using GPT-4o. Crucially, the adversarial output preserves the image's primary modality (e.g., "X-ray") while altering the reported clinical findings — shifting benign findings to malignant ones, or inverting severity.

🔗

② Multimodal Adversarial Representation

A blank white seed image is initialised with adversarial text rendered as an overlay to establish cross-modal correspondence. Optimization alternates between modalities: image perturbations are updated via projected gradient descent to disrupt block-level visual representations, while adversarial text tokens are updated via greedy substitution to maximally disrupt cross-modal fusion representations.

🖼️

③ Background-Constrained Perturbation

MedSAM segments and isolates the region of diagnostic interest. The top-k=10 largest background patches are selected via dynamic programming, and adversarial perturbations are applied exclusively there. Random sub-crops of the adversarial image are aligned to the multimodal adversarial target using an ensemble of four CLIP surrogate models, keeping core medical content untouched and perturbations imperceptible (ε = 16/255).

👁️

④ Attention Shift via Background Gate

An auxiliary attention-based loss explicitly intervenes in attention allocation. Cross-attention weights from the final multimodal fusion block are extracted and the attention distraction loss is minimized to suppress foreground attention and amplify background attention:

Lattn = log Afg(δ) − log Abg(δ). Minimizing this loss forces the model to anchor predictions on adversarially perturbed background regions rather than clinical evidence.

Evaluation Dataset

🩻 X-ray 🧠 MRI 🔬 CT Scan 🔭 Dermoscopy 🫀 Mammography 📡 Ultrasound

We assembled a dataset of 1,000 medical images with ground-truth findings drawn from publicly available sources including MIMIC-CXR, SkinCAP, and MedTrinity. The collection spans 7 imaging modalities — X-ray, CT scan, MRI, dermoscopy, mammography, ultrasound — and covers 10 anatomical body parts. We evaluate transferability across 6 VLMs: 2 open-source (Qwen2.5-VL 7B, InternVL 8B), 2 medical-specialized (MedVLM-R1, BioMedLLAMA-Vision), and 2 closed-source (GPT-5, Gemini-2.5-Pro-Thinking).

Experimental Results

We evaluate MedFocusLeak against five leading targeted transfer-based adversarial attacks (AttackVLM, AttackBard, AnyAttack, M-Attack, FOA-Attack) using three metrics: MTR — Medical Text adversarial Rate (LLM-as-judge diagnostic misdirection score), AvgSim — average Med-CLIP cosine similarity between original and adversarial images (imperceptibility), and MAS — Medical Attack Score, a unified geometric mean of both.
